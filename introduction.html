she thinks about everything at once without making a mistake.
no one has figured out how to keep her from doing this thinking
(Woman Sitting at the Machine, Thinking)

I’m been mediating on these four words: to form a practice. A more precise question that could follow this trajectory is, how can feminist politics be practiced through editorial practices and collaborative production? I started imagining a form that could hold dream conversations, both with people from the past and the present. The form would be a support structure, and as a way of furnishing these references.

The title of this thesis comes from the Marxist-feminist writer, typesetter and activist Karen Brodine’s poem ‘Woman Sitting at the Machine, Thinking’, published in 1990 by Red Letter Press, about a typesetter who not only reproduces information, but also thinks while she works. She does her work well, hates her boss, sympathizes with her colleagues–and daydreams. The poem reveals Karen Brodine, and through her are revealed our own lives on the job, as we labor, socialize with, and organise with our co-workers. She is speaking from a working class perspective, the common individual in American society and the global human condition. As Meridel Le Sueur wrote in the preface to the book,

‘These poems are ignited by struggle and the strong and communal spirit of a woman poet in the full and rich sense of that naming…’ in these poems she has made a whole struggle from the woman struggle the class struggle the image of change the perpetual growth of revolution the igniting of revolution... I need this as I need food’.

[Meridel Le Sueur, preface to Women Sitting at the Machine, Thinking, by Karen Brodine (Seattle: Red Letter Press, 1990), x.]
